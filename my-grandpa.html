Overview

How do you learn about a person living a life before you?

When a person is alive, it is possible to interact, work or live with him/her. Since these are direct interactions with the person, I consider these to be first-hand accounts of a person's life. On the other hand, people's deeds continue to survive after death in various narrative forms: oral accounts, books, photographs, videos, etc. Since media cannot be equated with the person themselves, I consider these second-hand accounts. Through these media, a person collapses from a vivid whole into a few flat impressions. This defect seemed inevitable before the invention of the time machine.

However, second-hand accounts have their unique charm. When communicating with people, I find it interesting that different people can have different versions of a story or entirely opposite comments on a person. These differences seem to urge me to choose: What is the truth? Or, which story should I believe? As a result, the once truth becomes a myth, and what I choose to believe is nothing more than a reflection of my mindset. This is one of the most interesting findings during the production of this interactive story.

Inspiration

This novel was inspired by my impressions of my grandfather, who died when I was only four years old. Although he is long gone, I occasionally hear stories about him. Sometimes I would find items in my home that proved he had existed. Just like fairytales, my grandpa's stories are mysterious, and they keep me thinking about what kind of person my grandpa was. Unlike fairytales, I have tangible evidence that proves his once existence. Upon writing this story, I came to believe that most people live ordinary lives, but we always have the choice to discover some extraordinary from the ordinary.

Production

Research

To better understand interactivity storytelling, I researched narrativity and interactivity in games. According to Ryan's book Avatars of Story, there are four types of plots and four types of interactivity:

Plot types Interactivity
State-transition diagram External-exploratory
The interplay of actual and virtual events Internal-exploratory
Interwoven destiny lines External-ontological
Travel in the story world Internal-ontological

In my essay Avatars in A Plague Tale: Innocence, I analyzed the narrativity and interactivity of the game A Plague Tale: Innocence.

Build the story

The interactive novel My Grandpa was built using the Twine engine, an excellent tool for hypertext story writing. The novel writes about a girl trying to get to know her grandpa, who has passed away, by finding his legacies and listening to his stories from other family members.

Since I wanted to recreate the experience of collecting evidence and solving 'mysteries' about grandpa, I planned my novel to be internal-exploratory with the plot type of 'travel in the story world'. The novel has one beginning, one end, and multiple nodes in between for players to make decisions or explore. In this way, players match themselves to the girl in the novel and explore the story world together with her.
